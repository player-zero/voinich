# A reproducible statistical investigation of the Voynich manuscript

Seventeen pre-registered statistical tests, five generative models, and an
attempt to refute our own conclusion — all runnable with one command each,
pure Python standard library, no dependencies.

**Verdict (by weight of evidence, not proof):** the text is not a natural
language, not a cipher of one, and not a code of coherent content. Every
measurable property of it is generated by the mechanics of skilled,
habit-driven writing. Form is hyper-organized at every scale; information
flows across none of the joints.

## Provenance — read this first

This investigation was directed by an amateur: a working mid-level
programmer with an interest in languages and no background in linguistics,
cryptology, or manuscript studies. All code, tests, and analysis were
produced in collaboration with an AI assistant (Claude, by Anthropic): the
human set the directions and asked the questions ("do it from scratch,
ignore prior decipherment attempts", "now try to refute our own verdict",
"re-run everything per section"), the AI designed the tests, wrote the
code, and ran them.

We state this upfront so you know exactly what you are dealing with — and
it is why the repository is built the way it is. Nothing here asks to be
taken on authority: every number is one command away from your own
machine, the falsification criteria are explicit, and the "How to refute
this work" section is meant literally. If the work is wrong, it should be
easy to show; we would welcome that outcome as much as confirmation.

## Three numbers that carry the argument

| Measure | Voynich | Natural-language control | Meaning |
|---|---|---|---|
| Mutual information between adjacent words | **0.31 bits** | 1.65 bits (herbal English, same size) | 3–5× below *any* direct encoding of prose; the residual decomposes fully into writing mechanics |
| Conditional character entropy H2 | **2.13 bits** | 3.24 bits | more predictable than any known language; every cipher family requires H2 ≥ 3 (substitution preserves it, all others raise it) |
| 4-word phrases repeated across distant pages | **1** | 2,646 | recipe books, prayers, reference texts all repeat formulas; this text never does |

## The single most telling experiment

The strongest inter-word dependency in the text — after a word ending in
`-y`, the next word starts with `qo-` 26% of the time (vs 6–9% after
consonants, a 4.1× pull) — **dies at line breaks** (15.6% vs 13.6%, pull
1.1×). Rules of grammar and rules of ciphers do not know where a line ends.
A pen does. See `scripts/test13_falsify.py`.

## Quick start

```sh
sh scripts/fetch_data.sh        # downloads transliteration + control text
python3 scripts/basic_stats.py  # any script runs standalone from repo root
python3 scripts/slot_grammar.py
python3 scripts/arbitrage.py    # the generator showdown (a few minutes)
```

Python 3.9+, no third-party packages. (`scripts/make_report.py` alone needs
`reportlab` and uses macOS system fonts.)

Checkpoints you should reproduce: H2 ≈ 2.13, slot-grammar coverage ≈ 90%,
adjacent-word MI excess ≈ 0.31 bits, exactly one cross-page 4-gram,
qo-pull ×4 inside lines vs ×1.1 across line breaks.

## What is replicated vs what is new

We deliberately worked "from scratch": hypotheses were formulated and
tested blind, with controls (a same-size 17th-century English herbal, and
structure-preserving shuffles), before comparing with the literature.
Consequently much of this is **independent replication** of known results,
which we credit here:

- low character-level entropy: Bennett (1976);
- rigid word structure / slot paradigms: Stolfi; Currier's languages A/B;
- self-citation / autocopying: Timm & Schinner (2019) — our word-level test
  *weakens* the strong form of their hypothesis while supporting the
  broader generated-text conclusion;
- table-and-grille generation: Rugg (2004) — our implementation of it
  fails Zipf and repetition tests;
- scribal hands: Lisa Fagin Davis; transliteration and page variables:
  Zandbergen & Landini;
- humans writing pseudo-text reproduce Voynich-like statistics:
  Gaskell & Bowern (2022);
- the alphagram hypothesis we test and reject on word-order grounds:
  Hauer & Kondrak (2016).

Elements we have not seen elsewhere in this form: the line-break mortality
of the `qo` cross-space rule; the full decomposition of the 0.31-bit MI
residual into split-words + pen flow; the constructive five-model
arbitrage on the manuscript's own page skeleton; the habit-vs-payload
analysis of "free choices" with an explicit ~4 KB steganographic capacity
ceiling; frequency-band burstiness showing the absence of function-word
anchors. We would genuinely like to know if any of these have priors.

## Post-audit patches (added after an adversarial self-review)

We audited our own weakest points and ran three patch tests
(`test18..20`):

- **Wider language yardsticks.** Latin (Caesar, free word order):
  H2 = 3.30, adjacent-word MI excess = 0.97 — so the honest gap to the
  loosest natural-language baseline is ~3×, not the 5× the English
  control suggested. Maori (14-letter alphabet, strict CV syllables):
  H2 = 2.53 on a small sample — the closest any language gets to the
  manuscript's 2.13; small-inventory languages narrow that particular
  gap, and we say so plainly.
- **The 166 cross-page 3-grams autopsied.** 4.7× above the
  within-page-shuffle null, but 94% composed of top-100 words, 1% rare
  words: stock chains of favorite words (perseveration), not
  content-bearing formulas.
- **Scribal line-justification refuted as an alternative.** Last-word
  length does not anti-correlate with line fullness (r = +0.04, vs
  +0.15 for mid-line words), and the -m flourish rate is flat across
  fullness terciles: the edge effects are positional ritual, not
  fitting words to the margin.

## Limitations, honestly

- Absence of meaning is unprovable in principle; this is a
  weight-of-evidence verdict with explicit falsification criteria.
- A steganographic payload *whitened to mimic habit statistics* is
  unfalsifiable by these methods; its capacity is bounded (~4 KB total).
- A cipher of inherently non-coherent content (a registry of unique
  codes) survives the MI tests, but explains neither layout-dependent
  word forms nor pen-flow effects.
- We take the ZL transliteration as ground truth; key results are stable
  under the Takahashi transliteration, but we did no glyph-level work on
  the scans themselves.

## How to refute this work

1. Find any section or layer with adjacent-word MI near 1 bit — the
   verdict collapses.
2. Find 50+ four-word phrases repeated across distant pages — it collapses.
3. Find a word-form rule that survives line breaks as strongly as it works
   inside lines — it is badly shaken.
4. Exhibit any language with per-character H2 ≈ 2.1 bits — the door to
   simple substitution reopens. (Closest we measured: Maori at 2.53 on
   a small sample; see the post-audit patches section.)
